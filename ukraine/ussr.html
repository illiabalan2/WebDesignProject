Overview of the Trypillian Culture

The Trypillian culture, also known as the Cucuteni-Trypillia culture, flourished during the Neolithic period between 5500 BCE and 2750 BCE in modern-day Ukraine, Moldova, and Romania. Known for advanced agriculture, distinctive pottery, and large settlements.

Architecture and Settlements

The Trypillian people were known for building large, well-planned settlements in circular patterns. Their houses were made from wattle and daub, and some settlements housed up to 15,000 people.

Artifacts and Pottery

The Trypillians produced distinctive pottery with geometric patterns, as well as figurines believed to represent fertility deities, and tools made from stone, bone, and clay.

Social Structure and Economy

The Trypillian society was agrarian, with a focus on farming and livestock. They lived in a relatively egalitarian society and engaged in trade with neighboring cultures.

Legacy of the Trypillians

Though the Trypillian culture disappeared around 2750 BCE, their influence can still be seen in modern archaeology, especially in the design of large settlements and distinctive pottery.
